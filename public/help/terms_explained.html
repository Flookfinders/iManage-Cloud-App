What is BS7666?

BS7666 is a British Standard that sets out the guidelines upon which the NLPG is built. It contains standards by which to record Basic Land and Property Units (BLPUs), which include within them a Unique Property Reference Number (UPRN), grid co-ordinates and one or more Land Property Identifiers (LPIs).

It is BS7666 standardisation that enables the amalgamation of all local authority address data into the NLPG and is the standard that makes accurate and efficient address management possible.

BS7666 - Addressable Objects

BS7666 lays down guidelines for the creation and management of address objects, which are real world objects that have fixed locations and may be identified and referenced by one or more addresses.

Addressable Objects are divided into two categories:

•Primary Addressable Objects (PAO), which is the name given to an addressable object that does not require referencing to another addressable object e.g. house number or building name.

•Secondary Addressable Objects (SAO), which is the name given when an addressable object is referenced to a PAO e.g. Flat 3 (SAO), Wellington House (PAO).

BS7666 - Non-Addressable Objects

Unlike many other address databases, the BS7666 standard enables the creation and management of non-addressable objects such as communication masts. These non-addressable objects form an essential component within the NLPG, and through the BS7666 standard, they can be held alongside postal addresses. This means they can be accessed simultaneously, cross referenced etc.

BS7666 - Precise, consistent formatting

The exact formatting that is derived through BS7666 means that the components that make up an address will be consistent throughout. This makes division by postcode, county locality or postal town quick, easy and accurate.
What is the NSG?

The National Street Gazetteer (NSG) is a database that holds information about all the streets in England and Wales.  In addition to the name of the street it contains information such as its type, location and length. It is compiled from the Local Street Gazetteers (LSGs) that are created by each of the 172 Highway Authorities in England and Wales and is an essential tool in the managing of street works.

The information from the National Street Gazetteer forms the basis of the information within the National Land and Property Gazetteer (NLPG) as it is only from the core street information that property information can be placed. It is also used to plan streetworks and enables the minimising of disruption through the information being available from one, complete source. Designed to be an unambiguous referencing system with which to identify any length of ‘Street’ in Great Britain the gazetteer is made available through a partnership with the local authorities.

The National Street Gazetteer is currently managed by private firm GeoPlace on behalf of the Improvement and Development Agency (IDeA), which is a government organisation that coordinates and promotes good practice within local authorities.

Flexibility

The National Street Gazetteer will be provided at three levels of specification.

The level 1 specification consists of streets described by their name, classification or general description and provided with a unique reference number. In addition each street will be given its spatial location through the National Grid reference to its start and finish. The names of the locality, town and county, within which the street is located, are also provided.

Levels 2 and 3 of the National Street Gazetteer provide further information about the positioning of intermediate street junctions and the complete geography of the streets.

Applications

This high quality base level information can be used in applications such as:

•Street works registration systems

•road traffic congestion systems

•market analysis and targeting

•highway maintenance

•linking and integrating layers of data within a single function

•unambiguous linking between databases
What are Streets?

Each object within the gazetteer is referenced to a Street. A street is described by a combination of street name or street descriptor records, locality and town. Where multiple languages are in use each street record must have appropriate street descriptors. Each street has a Unique Street Reference Number (USRN) that identifies a street chosen from a range provided by the National Gazetteer Custodian. All other information about a street, such as ESUs, ASD etc. are linked to the Street using the USRN.

A street record contains the following information:

•Unique Street Reference Number (USRN)

•One of the four street type (named, described, numbered, unoffical)

•start and end coordinates of the street

•information relating to the current status within the lifecycle of the identifier

•metadata relating to the maintenance of the record.

A street descriptor record contains the following information:

•Unique Street Reference Number

•street descriptor (street name, description or number)

•locality (optional)

•town

•administrative Area

•language of identifier (English / Welsh)
What are ESUs?

Streets are divided into sections usually defined by intersecting roads which are called Elementary Street Units (ESUs). A Street is a collection of ESUs. The ESUs define the geometry of the street and the direction of each element of the street e.g. One-way, Two-way.

What are One-way Exemptions?

A One-way exemption (OWE) can be applied to any one-way ESU. An exemption can be used to record contra-flow lanes where one type of traffic can travel in the opposite direction from the one-way restriction, for example buses or emergency vehicles.
What are Highway Dedications?

The Highway Dedication record holds a description of who is allowed to use a street, for this section of ESU together, with seasonal information and whether or not the ESU is subject to certain restrictions such as being a Public Right of Way.
What is ASD?

Associated Street Data (ASD) forms part of the National Street Gazetteer (NSG) and contains a wealth of additional information related to streets.  The Associated Street Data contains information such as the ownership of the street, streets with special surfaces, height and weight restrictions, traffic sensitive streets and much more.  Associated Street Data significantly increases the scope of the National Street Gazetteer by allowing those using it to have all the information about any particular street easily available.

There are five types of ASD:

•Interest Records – who maintains or has an interest in the street (or part of the street)

•Construction Records – the basic construction of the street in terms of traffic levels and any special surfaces

•Height, Weight and Width Restrictions

•Special Designation Records – a wealth of additional information

•Public Rights of Way (PRoW)

As with the NSG, all Associated Street Data complies with BS7666.
What is a PRoW?

A PRoW (Public Right of Way) is a right by which any member of the public may travel across land. There are several kinds of 'way' over which the public have a right to pass and they are collectively known as highways. The nature of the right depends on the nature of the highway.

•Footpath - right of way on foot only.

•Bridleway - right of way on foot, horseback and on pedal cycle.

•Restricted Byway – right of way on foot, horseback and non-mechanically propelled vehicles which, includes such things as pushbikes and horse and carriage but not motor vehicles.

•Byway open to all traffic (BOAT) - right of way on foot, horseback, pedal cycle and wheeled vehicles of all kinds including horse drawn vehicles. (These are generally used by the public for walking and riding)

•Cycle Track or Cycle Way - paths with a right of way for all types of pedal cycles (not mopeds), including electrically-assisted cycles, with or without a right of access on foot.

•Permissive Path - A permissive path, permitted path or concessionary path is not a public right of way, but a path (which could be for walkers, riders, cyclists, or any combination) whose use is allowed by the landowner, but over which there is no legal right of access. Some permissive footpaths and bridleways are shown on 1:25,000 and 1:50,00 scale Ordnance Survey maps.

A permissive path is often closed on a specified calendar day each year, and clearly signed as permissive. These are precautions to prevent any possible future claim of continuous public access, which could result in its becoming designated as a statutory right of way.

Public rights of way form an important recreational resource for members of the public but also provide a means of access to local facilities such as shops and bus stops.
What is the NLPG?

The National Land & Property Gazetteer (NLPG) contains a comprehensive list of property in England and Wales, including over 23 million residential properties and 1.5 million commercial locations, together with 4.6 million Objects Without Postal Addresses (OWPAs).

Each object contained in the gazetteer is described by means of:

•a unique identifier (UPRN)

•one or more address labels

•an easting and northing coordinate pair

•information relating to the lifecycle of the object

•business name

•a building classification.

This allows the data to be held and used in relational database and Geographical Information Systems (GIS Graphical Information Systems) to underpin a variety of applications.

The NLPG is fully compatible with other available geographical datasets via spatial relationships or identifier lookups including:

•The National Street Gazetteer (NSG)

•Vector mapping products

•Aerial imagery and photography

•LIDAR data

•Valuation Office Agency datasets.

The NLPG covers the whole of England and Wales and is a central repository or “hub” of gazetteer information created and maintained by local authorities.

Furthermore, the NLPG has rigorous data management processes (incorporating established best practice techniques) employed by local government to ensure the quality and efficiency not only of its address management, but of service delivery to citizens throughout the organisation.

What is a BLPU?

The Basic Land and Property Unit [BLPU] represents the real world objects recorded within the gazetteer and can be thought of as relating to “properties” or “locations”, such as a physical unit with a dwelling with its own address, property centroid and mapped boundary (provenance). Each Property (BLPU) has a Unique Property Reference Number (UPRN) that identifies a property chosen from a range provided by the National Gazetteer Custodian. All other information about a property, such as addresses, cross references etc. are linked to the BLPU using the UPRN.

The BLPU contains the following information:

•Unique Property Reference Number (UPRN)

•an easting and northing coordinate to describe the location of the object

•information relating to the current status within the lifecycle of the object

•a three tier classification system

•an organisation name (optional)

•Ward and Parish information (optional)

•metadata relating to the maintenance of the record

•a BLPU must be referenced to one or more LPI .
What are Children / Grandchildren?

Children, grand children and great grandchildren are used in situations when there are many addresses within the same property or many properties within a property. For example, a block of flats or a large estate with many different buildings. All the flats or buildings share the same street address (BLPU Parent) but have an additional address such as a flat number or building name (BLPU Child) to uniquely identify it within the building or estate.
What are LPIs?

The Land & Property Identifier (LPI) is made up of a Primary Addressable Object (PAO), Secondary Addressable Object (SAO) and Street Reference USRN. Properties are usually identified by their street address e.g. 41 High Street. However, if there were a hotel on the property it may also be identified by the name of the hotel, such as "The White Horse". Properties may be assigned an official name [Approved Official LPI], usually the street address as well as "Other" names [Alternative LPI] to make it easier to identify. An LPI must be referenced to a single BLPU.

An LPI contains the following information:

•LPI_KEY (primary key for LPIs)

•Unique Property Reference Number (UPRN) - used to reference to the corresponding BLPU

•Unique Street Reference Number (USRN) – used to reference to corresponding street

•the language of the identifier

•information relating to the current status within the lifecycle of the identifier

•the property/location name and/or number details

•postal information – Postcode / Posttown (where required)

•metadata relating to the maintenance of the record

 
What are Provenances?

Provenances are the basis for the definition of the polygon that represents the extent of the BLPU and delineate the land parcel boundary of the BLPU and other boundaries including building outlines, property extents, rights of way. Also differences in occupancy such as public, private, abandoned and land-uses such as allotments, waste dumps and nature reserves. Multiple provenance boundaries may be recorded for each BLPU point. Each boundary (BLPU extent) is assigned a provenance code. For example the provenance code for parcel boundaries is "Physical Features".

What are Cross-References?

Cross-references are unique identifiers that link the property (BLPU) to external applications - e.g. electoral role, council tax, address-point data. Cross-references are important because of the need to synchronise addresses back to the core systems that were initially used to build the LLPG. It is imperative to keep cross-references accurate as, in some instances, they will be used to ascertain which address records in the external system should be changed. It is usual to find an Electoral Registration, Council Tax and Address-Point cross-reference against all residential properties. Should you find residential properties missing one of these core cross-references this would indicate a data error that you would wish to resolve.
What is an OWPA?

An Object Without Postal Address  [OWPA] is exactly what it says on the tin, an object without a postal address. Usually, when people consider addresses they think purely of residential properties or properties which receive mail. However, in addition to comprehensive sets of these records, the NLPG scope extends far beyond this and includes many OWPAs.

These include for example:

•street BLPU records (used to record where incidents such as road traffic accidents may occur)

•Agricultural

•industrial

•transport and utility infrastructure

•land

•amenities such as car parks and public conveniences

•objects of interest such as monuments and places of worship.

Each LPI entry has a flag to indicate whether or not it is postally-addressable. For each postally-addressable object the relevant information to generate both geographic and postal addresses will be populated. For non postally-adddressable objects, the geographic address information will be provided.
